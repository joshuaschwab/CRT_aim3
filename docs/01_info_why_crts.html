When you need a CRT

Some interventions act at the group level — village health teams, clinic care-delivery models, public-health campaigns — and cannot be delivered to an individual in isolation. For these, the only valid randomized comparison randomizes groups. Examples:

  • Universal HIV test-and-treat (SEARCH): 32 communities in Kenya and Uganda randomized to a community-level prevention package vs. standard-of-care.
  • Dynamic Choice HIV Prevention (SAPPHIRE, Peer-led DCP): 16 and 14 communities respectively, offering choice-based PrEP/PEP delivery.
  • Isoniazid preventive therapy (SEARCH-IPT): district-randomized trial to improve IPT uptake.

CRTs preserve the rigor of randomization while letting researchers ask questions that map directly to how interventions are deployed in the real world (Balzer et al. 2021). They are increasingly the design of choice for translating efficacy into effectiveness.

What makes CRTs hard

Compared to individually-randomized trials, CRTs face three persistent challenges:

Few independent units

Most CRTs randomize fewer than 40 clusters. With small numbers of independent units, the dependence among participants within a cluster sharply reduces the effective sample size, and standard inferential tools (mixed models, generalized estimating equations) tend to perform poorly with finite-sample corrections.

Missing outcomes

One review found 93% of CRTs had missing outcomes. Naïve complete-case analyses are biased when participants with measured outcomes differ meaningfully from those with missed outcomes. The problem is worse when the missingness mechanism is itself influenced by the intervention — e.g., when the intervention’s first action is to extend screening.

Effect heterogeneity

Fewer than 30% of CRTs assess effect heterogeneity, because practical guidance on such analyses has been lacking (Balzer et al. 2021). Established subgroups (sex, age group, region) provide some insight, but miss the chance to identify multilevel factors that predict who benefits most.

Where this site fits

The remaining chapters build a single coherent methodology that addresses all three challenges — anchored on the Causal Roadmap (Chapter 2) and implemented in the TwoStageTMLE R package.
