The big idea

Two-Stage TMLE separates missing-data adjustment from between-arm comparison:

Stage What it does Independent unit Estimator
1 Adjust for missing outcomes / mediated missingness within each cluster Individual TMLE via ltmle
2 Compare cluster-level endpoints between arms Cluster TMLE with Adaptive Pre-specification

The separation is the whole point. Stage 1 handles the messy individual- level data without trying to estimate the effect; Stage 2 sees clean cluster-level data and runs the comparison with maximum efficiency.

Stage 1: within-cluster missing-data adjustment

For each cluster \(c\) separately, fit an individual-level TMLE for the cluster-specific endpoint \(Y^c\). Two variants:

Foundational (missing outcomes)

Used when the outcome itself is differentially measured — e.g., viral suppression status known only for those who returned for follow-up.

\[ Y^c \;=\; \mathbb{E}\!\bigl[\mathbb{E}(Y \mid \Delta = 1, W, M)\bigr] \]

where \(\Delta\) is the outcome-measurement indicator and \((W, M)\) are baseline plus post-baseline covariates. One \(\mathrm{ltmle}\) call per cluster, returning one cluster-level number. See Chapter 5 for hands-on code.

Mediated (the multilevel-mediation-missing problem)

Used when eligibility / risk status is what’s partially missing, mediated by post-baseline screening — e.g., HIV risk status known only for those who got screened.

\[ Y^c \;=\; \frac{\mathbb{P}(Y_2 = 1)}{\mathbb{E}\!\bigl[\mathbb{P}(Y_1 = 1 \mid M = 1, W)\bigr]} \]

A ratio estimator: numerator empirical, denominator TMLE. See Chapter 6 for hands-on code.

Stage 2: cluster-level TMLE with Adaptive Pre-specification

Hand the \(J\) cluster-level endpoints to a cluster-level TMLE that compares treated to control:

\[ \Psi \;=\; \mathbb{E}\bigl[Y^c(1) - Y^c(0)\bigr] \]

Adaptive Pre-specification (APS) picks the adjustment variable that minimises the CV variance of the resulting TMLE, while preserving Type-I error control (Balzer et al. 2016). With few clusters, APS routinely yields 4–6× efficiency gains over an unadjusted estimator.

Why this is doubly robust and efficient

Both stages plug in machine-learning (SuperLearner) candidate fits. Stage 1 is doubly robust in the outcome regression and the \(\Delta\)-mechanism / \(M\)-mechanism. Stage 2 is doubly robust in the cluster-level outcome regression and the (known) randomization distribution.

When both are estimated well, Two-Stage TMLE is efficient — it achieves the smallest asymptotic variance among regular, asymptotically linear estimators.

On variance and finite-sample inference

Stage 2’s variance is computed from the cluster-level influence curve and treats Stage 1’s endpoint as a fixed observation at the cluster level. This is justified asymptotically when within-cluster sample sizes are large; see Box 2 of Balzer et al. (2021). Empirically, Nakato (2026) verifies acceptable coverage at \(N_c \approx 200\) per cluster, \(J \in \{20, 30, 50, 70\}\) — well within the regime of most HIV prevention CRTs.
