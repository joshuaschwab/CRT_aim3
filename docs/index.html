Welcome

Cluster randomized trials (CRTs) randomize groups — communities, clinics, schools, health systems — rather than individuals. They are the design of choice when interventions act at the group level: training community health workers, changing a clinic’s care-delivery model, deploying a public-health campaign. CRTs are increasingly used to evaluate HIV prevention strategies that aim to both reach a target population and improve outcomes among those reached.

Compared with individually-randomized trials, CRTs face three statistical challenges that standard methods handle poorly:

  1. Few independent units. Most CRTs randomize fewer than 40 clusters.
  2. Missing outcomes are nearly universal, and the missingness mechanism is often on the causal pathway (e.g., screening that itself depends on the intervention).
  3. Effect heterogeneity at the cluster-, sub-cluster-, and individual-level is rarely evaluated well — established subgroups miss data-driven insights, and naïve stratification is severely underpowered.

This site presents Two-Stage TMLE (Balzer et al. 2021) — a targeted machine-learning estimator that addresses all three challenges in a single framework. The estimator separates the per-cluster missing-data adjustment (Stage 1) from the between-cluster effect estimation (Stage 2), and is doubly robust, efficient, and preserves Type-I error control with few independent units.

What you will learn

By the end of this site, participants will be able to:

  1. State a CRT research question using the Causal Roadmap (Petersen and van der Laan 2014) and identify the multilevel data structure and missing-data mechanism.
  2. Run Two-Stage TMLE on a CRT with missing outcomes using the TwoStageTMLE R package — including Adaptive Pre-specification.
  3. Diagnose when the multilevel-mediation-missing data problem applies (post-baseline screening mediates the effect) and use the Nakato (2026) ratio-based Stage 1 estimator instead.
  4. Write a Statistical Analysis Plan (SAP) for a CRT analysis that pre-specifies the Super Learner library, the candidate adjustment set, and the inference scheme.

How to use this site

The chapters are organised in two arcs:

  • Foundations (chapters 1–3) — conceptual material: why CRTs need special methods, the Causal Roadmap, and the three core challenges.
  • Two-Stage TMLE (chapters 4–6) — the method itself, plus two hands-on chapters that run real code against simulated CRTs.

Each R-coded chapter is reproducible: code chunks are executable and the data live alongside the chapter. Reading time per chapter: ~20 minutes.
